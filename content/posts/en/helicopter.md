## What You Need

- 1 sheet of origami paper (15cm × 15cm)
- A flat surface
- Scissors (optional, for rotor details)

## Instructions

1. Place the paper white side up and fold it diagonally both ways to create creases, then unfold

![Step 1](/images/origami/steps/helicopter/step-1.svg)

2. Fold the paper in half horizontally, crease, and unfold

![Step 2](/images/origami/steps/helicopter/step-2.svg)

3. Collapse the paper into a preliminary base (triangle shape) using the existing creases

![Step 3](/images/origami/steps/helicopter/step-3.svg)

4. Fold the top layer's left and right corners to the center point

![Step 4](/images/origami/steps/helicopter/step-4.svg)

5. Unfold and perform a petal fold by lifting the bottom point upward

![Step 5](/images/origami/steps/helicopter/step-5.svg)

6. Flip the model over and repeat steps 4-5 on the other side

![Step 6](/images/origami/steps/helicopter/step-6.svg)

7. Fold the right flap of the top layer over to the left (like turning a book page)

![Step 7](/images/origami/steps/helicopter/step-7.svg)

8. Fold the bottom point upward to form the tail boom

![Step 8](/images/origami/steps/helicopter/step-8.svg)

9. Narrow the tail by folding its edges inward

![Step 9](/images/origami/steps/helicopter/step-9.svg)

10. Fold the tip of the tail to one side to create the tail rotor

![Step 10](/images/origami/steps/helicopter/step-10.svg)

11. Open the top layers and flatten them outward to form the main rotor blades

![Step 11](/images/origami/steps/helicopter/step-11.svg)

12. Gently curve each rotor blade by rolling it around a pencil

![Step 12](/images/origami/steps/helicopter/step-12.svg)

13. Shape the body by opening the bottom slightly so it can stand

![Step 13](/images/origami/steps/helicopter/step-13.svg)


## Tips

- **Rotor balance**: Make sure both rotor blades are the same length and angle for a balanced look
- **Tail shape**: A crisp reverse fold on the tail gives a more realistic silhouette
- **Dropping test**: Drop the helicopter from a height — well-curved rotors will make it spin as it falls
- **Paper size**: Larger paper (20cm+) makes the folding steps much easier

## Fun Fact

Leonardo da Vinci sketched an "aerial screw" flying machine in 1489, often considered the earliest concept of a helicopter. The first practical helicopter didn't fly until 1939. The paper helicopter (or "whirlybird") is actually one of the earliest STEM toys — its spinning descent demonstrates the principles of air resistance and angular momentum.
