## What You Need

- 5-6 sheets of origami paper (assorted colors, 7.5cm × 7.5cm or 15cm × 15cm)
- String or thread
- Tape or glue
- Scissors (optional)
- A flat surface

## Instructions (One Star)

1. Place the paper colored side down and fold it in half to make a rectangle

![Step 1](/images/origami/steps/tanabata/step-1.svg)

2. Fold the right half up at an angle so the bottom-right corner meets the top-left edge

![Step 2](/images/origami/steps/tanabata/step-2.svg)

3. Fold the left section behind and align it with the right edge

![Step 3](/images/origami/steps/tanabata/step-3.svg)

4. Cut at an angle across the folded layers, from the lower-left to the upper-right

![Step 4](/images/origami/steps/tanabata/step-4.svg)

5. Unfold carefully to reveal a five-pointed star

![Step 5](/images/origami/steps/tanabata/step-5.svg)

6. Press the star flat and sharpen the creases at each point

![Step 6](/images/origami/steps/tanabata/step-6.svg)


## Making the Garland

1. Fold 5 to 6 stars in different colors

![Step 7](/images/origami/steps/tanabata/step-7.svg)

2. Cut a length of string (about 60-80cm)

![Step 8](/images/origami/steps/tanabata/step-8.svg)

3. Tape or glue each star onto the string, spacing them evenly

![Step 9](/images/origami/steps/tanabata/step-9.svg)

4. Leave extra string at each end for hanging

![Step 10](/images/origami/steps/tanabata/step-10.svg)


## Tips

- **Paper size**: Smaller paper creates dainty stars; larger paper makes bold ones — mix sizes for variety
- **Cut angle**: A steep angle produces long, narrow points; a shallow angle makes wider, stubbier points
- **Add wishes**: Write a wish on the back of each star, following the Tanabata tradition
- **Bamboo display**: Tie the garland to a bamboo branch for an authentic Tanabata decoration

## Fun Fact

Tanabata celebrates the once-a-year meeting of the star-crossed lovers Orihime (Vega) and Hikoboshi (Altair), separated by the Milky Way. On July 7th, people write wishes on colorful paper strips called tanzaku and hang them on bamboo branches, hoping the stars will grant their dreams.
